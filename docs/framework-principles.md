# Framework Principles

**Digital Signal Governance — Documentation**  
Published by Shikhar Jha in association with Michvi LLP | Version 1.1.0

---

## Purpose

This document defines the core principles that guide the Digital Signal Governance (DSG) framework.

These principles establish how DSG should be understood, interpreted, extended, and applied as a conceptual governance framework.

---

## 1. Governance-First Principle

Digital Signal Governance treats signal-layer conditions as governance conditions.

DSG does not frame signal problems primarily as technical defects, analytics issues, vendor limitations, or operational errors. It frames them as governance conditions because they affect accountability, reliance, documentation, interpretation, consent, identity, and institutional decision-making.

---

## 2. Signal-Layer Principle

DSG is concerned with the signal layer: the conditions under which digital signals are generated, admitted, interpreted, propagated, and relied upon.

The signal layer exists upstream of reporting, attribution, optimization, automation, and executive interpretation.

Governance failure at the signal layer may affect every downstream function that depends upon those signals.

---

## 3. Upstream Orientation Principle

DSG places governance attention upstream of visible outputs.

Reports, dashboards, attribution models, and automated systems often present downstream results without revealing the governance conditions under which the underlying signals were formed.

DSG emphasizes governance visibility before downstream reliance becomes consequential.

---

## 4. Conceptual Independence Principle

DSG is independent of specific technologies, vendors, platforms, tools, and implementation approaches.

The framework defines governance conditions, not product categories or engineering mechanisms.

DSG terminology should remain stable even as technologies, platforms, and implementation patterns change.

---

## 5. Non-Operational Scope Principle

DSG does not define implementation logic, operational workflows, detection mechanisms, enforcement procedures, scoring methods, or technical architectures.

The framework provides conceptual vocabulary, taxonomy, boundary conditions, and governance reasoning structures.

Operational use of DSG concepts may occur in practice, but operational procedures are outside the scope of the public conceptual framework.

---

## 6. Boundary Discipline Principle

DSG must remain clearly distinguished from adjacent disciplines including analytics, compliance, privacy frameworks, attribution modeling, audit frameworks, reporting systems, measurement vendor categories, technical detection systems, and Design-Time Governance.

Adjacent disciplines may intersect with DSG, but they do not replace it.

Boundary discipline protects the conceptual clarity and long-term usefulness of the framework.

---

## 7. Documentation Before Reliance Principle

Governance conditions should be documented before consequential reliance is placed upon signals, measurements, or interpretations.

Where governance conditions are undocumented, downstream reliance carries unresolved governance uncertainty.

The absence of documentation is itself a governance condition.

---

## 8. Interpretive Integrity Principle

Signals require stable meaning across contexts of use.

A signal may be technically present while remaining governance-uncertain if its meaning, scope, attribution, or conditions of validity are not consistently understood.

DSG therefore treats interpretation as a governance concern, not merely a technical or analytical concern.

---

## 9. Consent and Identity Alignment Principle

Signal governance depends on the alignment of consent states and identity continuity.

Where consent states are inconsistent, stale, or unverifiable, signal reliance may carry governance exposure.

Where identity continuity fails, measurement, attribution, consent governance, and downstream reliance may inherit structural uncertainty.

---

## 10. Observability Principle

Governance conditions must be observable, verifiable, or otherwise documentable to support credible governance assurance.

Where governance conditions cannot be observed or verified, observability gaps arise.

Observability gaps are not merely technical blind spots; they are governance conditions that may create or amplify governance exposure.

---

## 11. Versioned Canonicality Principle

DSG terminology, taxonomy, and boundary definitions should be maintained through versioned canonical references.

Canonical definitions should not change informally.

Material changes to terminology, taxonomy, or boundary conditions should be documented through version history and reflected in the relevant repository files.

---

## 12. Public-Safe Conceptual Publication Principle

Public DSG materials should remain conceptual, definitional, taxonomic, and boundary-oriented.

Public documentation should not disclose proprietary implementation logic, operational methods, technical architectures, mathematical models, detection methods, scoring systems, or internal assessment procedures.

This principle preserves the distinction between the public DSG conceptual framework and any separate proprietary systems, methods, or intellectual property.

---

## Summary

The Digital Signal Governance framework is governed by principles of conceptual clarity, upstream orientation, signal-layer focus, boundary discipline, documentation, interpretive integrity, and public-safe publication.

These principles guide how DSG terminology is defined, how taxonomy branches are structured, how boundaries are maintained, and how future framework development should proceed.

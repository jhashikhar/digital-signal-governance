# Governance Assumptions

**Digital Signal Governance — Documentation**  
Published by Shikhar Jha in association with Michvi LLP | Version 1.0

---

## Purpose

This document defines the foundational assumptions upon which the Digital Signal Governance (DSG) framework is based.

All governance frameworks contain assumptions about the environments they describe. Making those assumptions explicit improves interpretive consistency, boundary clarity, and governance transparency.

These assumptions are not operational requirements, implementation rules, or normative prescriptions.

They describe the conceptual conditions under which DSG is intended to be understood and applied.

---

## Assumption 1: Signals Precede Reliance

DSG assumes that organizational reliance occurs downstream of signals.

Measurements, attributions, reporting outputs, operational decisions, and automated processes depend upon signals that have already been generated, admitted, interpreted, and propagated.

Governance conditions affecting signals therefore have the potential to influence all downstream reliance.

---

## Assumption 2: Governance Conditions Exist Independently of Awareness

DSG assumes that governance conditions may exist whether or not they are recognized, documented, monitored, or actively managed.

The absence of awareness does not imply the absence of a governance condition.

Unrecognized governance conditions may still influence organizational reliance.

---

## Assumption 3: Governance Conditions and Operational Conditions Are Distinct

DSG assumes that operational success does not necessarily imply governance adequacy.

Systems may continue to function, measurements may continue to be reported, and decisions may continue to be made even where governance conditions are degraded, undocumented, or unverifiable.

Governance conditions therefore require independent consideration.

---

## Assumption 4: Documentation May Differ From Reality

DSG assumes that documented governance intentions and actual governance conditions may diverge over time.

Policies, definitions, architectures, and governance assumptions may remain unchanged while operational environments evolve.

The existence of documentation alone does not establish governance adequacy.

---

## Assumption 5: Governance Conditions Change Over Time

DSG assumes that governance conditions are dynamic rather than static.

Signal environments evolve through organizational, procedural, technical, and environmental change.

Governance conditions that were once adequate may become inadequate if governance assumptions fail to evolve with the environment.

---

## Assumption 6: Downstream Outputs May Conceal Upstream Conditions

DSG assumes that governance failures at the signal layer may not be visible within downstream outputs.

Reports, dashboards, measurements, attributions, and operational outcomes may appear internally consistent even where underlying governance conditions are degraded.

The visibility of outputs should not be treated as evidence of governance adequacy.

---

## Assumption 7: Observability Is Limited

DSG assumes that some governance conditions may not be fully observable through available information, documentation, or reporting mechanisms.

Observability limitations may themselves create governance exposure.

The inability to observe a governance condition does not imply that the condition does not exist.

---

## Assumption 8: Reliance Creates Governance Consequence

DSG assumes that governance significance arises through reliance.

A governance condition becomes consequential when signals affected by that condition are relied upon for measurement, attribution, reporting, operational activity, oversight, or decision-making.

Reliance transforms governance conditions into governance exposure.

---

## Assumption 9: Identity, Consent, and Measurement Depend on Signals

DSG assumes that identity governance, consent governance, and measurement governance are dependent upon signals and signal-related conditions.

These governance domains may be analyzed independently for conceptual purposes, but they remain structurally connected through signal environments.

---

## Assumption 10: Governance Exposure May Be Latent

DSG assumes that governance exposure may exist before visible consequences emerge.

A governance condition may remain dormant, unnoticed, or operationally invisible until triggered by audit activity, organizational review, regulatory inquiry, structural change, or consequential reliance.

The absence of visible failure is not evidence of governance adequacy.

---

## Assumption 11: Conceptual Vocabulary Improves Governance Clarity

DSG assumes that governance conditions become easier to understand, discuss, assess, and document when supported by consistent terminology and taxonomy.

Conceptual vocabulary does not eliminate governance challenges, but it improves the ability of organizations to identify and reason about governance conditions.

---

## Assumption 12: Governance Conditions Are Framework-Agnostic

DSG assumes that governance conditions may exist regardless of technology stack, implementation approach, vendor selection, platform architecture, organizational structure, or operational model.

The framework therefore focuses on governance conditions themselves rather than on the mechanisms through which those conditions arise.

---

## Interpretive Guidance

These assumptions are intended to support consistent interpretation of DSG terminology, taxonomy, boundary documents, and future framework development.

They should be read as conceptual foundations rather than evaluative criteria.

The presence of an assumption within this document does not imply that a particular governance condition exists in any specific environment.

---

## Summary

DSG assumes that governance conditions exist at the signal layer, may evolve independently of documentation, may remain invisible within downstream outputs, and may create governance exposure through organizational reliance.

These assumptions provide the conceptual foundation upon which DSG terminology, taxonomy, boundary definitions, and governance reasoning are built.

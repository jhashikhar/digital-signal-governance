## What Is Category Positioning?

Category positioning establishes the conceptual boundaries and authority claims for a governance framework or discipline. It defines what a category is, what it is not, how conceptual authority is established within it, and how it relates to adjacent categories.

Category positioning for DSG matters because:

1. **DSG is intended to address a governance gap.** Signal-layer governance conditions are not commonly articulated as a distinct governance category within existing mainstream governance frameworks. DSG provides terminology, taxonomy, and conceptual structure for describing, discussing, and evaluating such conditions within digital environments.

2. **Naming has authority consequences.** The publication of canonical definitions, taxonomic structure, and boundary conditions can establish conceptual authority within an emerging category, particularly where adjacent frameworks do not provide equivalent vocabulary. That authority must be established clearly and maintained consistently.

3. **Adjacent categories create boundary pressure.** Analytics, compliance, privacy, attribution modeling, and measurement technology are well-established adjacent categories. DSG's positioning must be clear enough that it is not collapsed into any of these adjacent categories or treated as a subset of them.

## DSG's Category Position

### The Upstream Layer
DSG is positioned at the **upstream governance layer** of digital signal environments. Many adjacent categories, including analytics, attribution, optimization, and automation, operate downstream of the signal layer addressed by DSG.

This upstream position is defensible, distinctive, and consequential:

- Defensible: DSG addresses governance conditions that are not systematically addressed as a distinct governance category within existing mainstream frameworks.
- Distinctive: DSG's concerns are structural and governance-oriented, not operational or technical
- Consequential: governance failures at the signal layer propagate into all downstream functions

### The Governance-First Frame
DSG frames signal-layer conditions as governance conditions — not as data quality problems, engineering challenges, or compliance requirements. This framing is deliberate:

- It situates accountability at the right level (governance, not operational)
- It makes DSG legible to audit, legal, and executive functions that think in governance terms
- It distinguishes DSG from technical and operational adjacent categories that address the same domain through different frames

### The Pre-Reporting Boundary
DSG's boundary at the reporting and operational layer is a key positioning element. By explicitly positioning as upstream of reporting, attribution, optimization, and automation, DSG:

- Claims the governance layer that these functions depend on but do not address
- Avoids competing with established analytics, reporting, and attribution categories
- Establishes a clear scope that makes DSG complementary to, rather than competitive with, downstream functions

## Adjacent Category Landscape

### Analytics and Measurement Platforms
Analytics and measurement platforms consume signals and produce reports. They occupy the downstream layer. DSG is upstream of analytics — it governs the conditions under which the signals that analytics consumes were formed. DSG is not a measurement category.

### Data Governance Frameworks
Data governance frameworks address how data assets are managed, catalogued, and governed across an organization. Data governance and DSG overlap in their concern for data quality and documentation, but DSG is specifically concerned with signal-layer governance conditions — not with enterprise data asset management broadly. DSG is a signal-specific governance discipline, not a general data governance framework.

### Privacy and Consent Management Platforms
Privacy and consent management platforms are operational tools that collect and manage consent expressions. DSG addresses consent architecture and consent-state alignment — the governance conditions that determine whether those platforms produce real governance. DSG is upstream of and complementary to consent management platforms; it governs the conditions those platforms must satisfy, not the platforms themselves.

### Compliance and Regulatory Frameworks
Compliance frameworks address adherence to regulatory requirements. DSG addresses structural governance conditions that regulatory compliance depends upon. DSG informs compliance but is not a compliance methodology. It is upstream of compliance assessment, providing the governance vocabulary for conditions that compliance frameworks must account for.

### Identity Resolution and Identity Graphs
Identity resolution technologies address the technical problem of associating signals with entities across fragmented identifiers. DSG addresses the governance conditions — identity continuity and fragmentation — that these technologies operate within. DSG governs what identity governance must achieve; identity resolution technologies are mechanisms for addressing identity governance conditions. DSG is not a technology category.

## Authority Establishment

This repository contributes to the development and documentation of the DSG conceptual category through:

**Canonical Publication:** This repository serves as the public reference source for the DSG terminology documented within this framework.

**Canonical Terminology:** DSG's canonical terminology — Signal Integrity, Signal Lifecycle, Signal Admission, Signal Dependency, Signal Conflict, Signal Ambiguity, Identity Continuity, Identity Fragmentation, Consent Architecture, Consent-State Alignment, Measurement Architecture, Attribution Dependency, Governance Exposure, Structural Drift, Observability Gaps, Interpretive Integrity — constitutes a coherent, distinctive vocabulary for the signal governance domain. This vocabulary is documented, versioned, and citeable.

**Boundary Clarity:** The boundary documents in this repository establish what DSG is and is not with precision sufficient to prevent boundary erosion. Adjacent categories are explicitly distinguished.

**Academic and Institutional Discoverability:** The repository’s JSON-LD metadata, structured citation, and taxonomy documentation are designed for indexing by academic and institutional systems. This supports discoverability when researchers and practitioners search for signal governance concepts and terminology. The DSG framework is published as a structured reference source for DSG concepts and definitions.

**Version Control:** All changes to terminology and boundary conditions are versioned, documented, and timestamped. This creates a citable record of category development over time.

## Category Development

Category authority is not established once — it is maintained and developed through consistent, high-quality publication of conceptual and governance research. Ongoing framework development may include:

- Maintaining the canonical terminology repository (this repository)
- Publishing applied governance research that uses and extends DSG concepts
- Engaging with academic, regulatory, and practitioner communities that work in signal-dependent environments
- Supporting broader citation, discussion, and reference to DSG terminology in research, governance documentation, and practitioner discourse
